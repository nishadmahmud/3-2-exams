> **Answer:**
> **1. Summary Table**
> 
> | $x_i$ | $y_i$ | $x_i^2$ | $x_i y_i$ |

> | 1 | 2.1 | 1 | 2.1 |

> 
> **2. Calculate Regression Coefficients**
> Using $n = 6$, $\bar{x} = 3.5$, $\bar{y} = 17.4/6 = 2.90$
>

> 
> **3. Regression Equation**
> $$\hat{y} = 1.381 + 0.434x$$
> 
> **4. Predictions**
> - **Week 8:** $\hat{y} = 1.381 + 0.434 \times 8 = 1.381 + 3.472 = \mathbf{4.85}$ (thousand dollars)
> - **Week 12:** $\hat{y} = 1.381 + 0.434 \times 12 = 1.381 + 5.208 = \mathbf{6.59}$ (thousand dollars)

(a) Briefly discuss about Q-Learning algorithm of Reinforcement Learning. **(4)**

> **Answer:**
> **Q-Learning** is a model-free Reinforcement Learning algorithm that teaches an agent to find the optimal action-selection policy by learning a Q-table (action-value function).
> 
> **Key Concepts:**
> - **Agent:** The learner/decision maker
> - **Environment:** The world the agent interacts with
> - **State (S):** Current situation of the agent
> - **Action (A):** Choices available at each state
> - **Reward (R):** Feedback received after taking an action
> - **Q-Table:** A table storing Q-values for every (State, Action) pair
> 
> **Q-Value Update Rule (Bellman Equation):**
> $$Q(S, A) \leftarrow Q(S, A) + \alpha \left[ R + \gamma \max_{a'} Q(S', a') - Q(S, A) \right]$$
> Where: $\alpha$ = learning rate, $\gamma$ = discount factor, $S'$ = next state
> 
> **Algorithm Steps:**
> 1. Initialize Q-table with zeros for all (S, A) pairs
> 2. Observe current state $S$
> 3. Choose action $A$ (using ε-greedy: explore randomly or exploit best Q)
> 4. Execute $A$, receive reward $R$, observe next state $S'$
> 5. Update Q-table using Bellman equation
> 6. Set $S \leftarrow S'$ and repeat until convergence

(b) Explain the steps to build an NLP pipeline. **(4)**

> **Answer:**
> An **NLP Pipeline** is a sequence of processing steps that transforms raw text into structured, machine-understandable data.
> 
> **Steps:**
> 1. **Text Collection / Input** — Gather raw text data from sources (documents, web, speech).
> 2. **Tokenization** — Split text into individual tokens (words, punctuation, sentences). *e.g., "I love data" → ["I", "love", "data"]*
> 3. **Stop Word Removal** — Remove common, low-information words (e.g., "the", "is", "and").
> 4. **Stemming / Lemmatization** — Reduce words to their root form. *e.g., "running" → "run"*
> 5. **Part-of-Speech (POS) Tagging** — Label each token with its grammatical role (noun, verb, adjective, etc.).
> 6. **Named Entity Recognition (NER)** — Identify and classify named entities (persons, places, dates, organizations).
> 7. **Feature Extraction** — Convert text to numerical representations (e.g., TF-IDF, Word2Vec, embeddings).
> 8. **Model Training / Classification** — Feed features into an ML/DL model for the target task (sentiment analysis, translation, etc.).

(a) What are the key challenges in Natural Language Processing? **(3)**

> **Answer:**
> **Key Challenges in NLP:**
> 1. **Ambiguity** — Words and sentences can have multiple meanings depending on context. *e.g., "Bank" (river bank vs. financial bank).*
> 2. **Context Understanding** — Machines struggle to understand the full context of long-range dependencies in text (sarcasm, idioms, metaphors).
> 3. **Sarcasm and Irony** — Detecting opposite-of-literal meanings is extremely difficult. *e.g., "Oh great, another Monday!"*
> 4. **Multilingualism** — NLP systems must handle thousands of languages, dialects, and scripts with varying grammar rules.
> 5. **Data Sparsity** — Many languages and domains lack sufficient labeled training data for effective model learning.

(b) Explain how collaborative filtering works. Illustrate with a sample user-item matrix. **(7)**

> **Answer:**
> **Collaborative Filtering (CF)** is a recommendation technique that makes predictions about a user's preferences based on the preferences of many other users. The core idea: *"Users who agreed in the past tend to agree in the future."*
> 
> **Types:**
> - **User-Based CF:** Find users similar to the target user, then recommend items those similar users liked.
> - **Item-Based CF:** Find items similar to what the target user has rated highly, then recommend those similar items.
> 
> **Sample User-Item Matrix (Ratings 1–5, '?' = unknown):**
> 
> | User | Movie A | Movie B | Movie C | Movie D |
> | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: |
> | User 1 | 5 | 3 | ? | 1 |
> | User 2 | 4 | ? | 4 | 1 |
> | User 3 | 1 | 1 | ? | 5 |
> | User 4 | ? | 4 | 5 | ? |
> 
> **How it works (User-Based Example):**
> 1. **Compute Similarity** between User 1 and all other users using Cosine Similarity or Pearson Correlation.
>    $$\text{similarity}(U_1, U_2) = \frac{\sum r_{1i} \cdot r_{2i}}{\sqrt{\sum r_{1i}^2} \cdot \sqrt{\sum r_{2i}^2}}$$
> 2. **Find K most similar users** to User 1 (e.g., User 2 is most similar with high ratings on A).
> 3. **Predict the missing rating** for User 1 on Movie C using weighted average of similar users' ratings:
>    $$\hat{r}_{1C} = \frac{\sum_{u \in N} \text{sim}(1,u) \cdot r_{uC}}{\sum_{u \in N} |\text{sim}(1,u)|}$$
> 4. **Recommend** the highest predicted-rating items that User 1 hasn't seen yet.
> 
> **Advantages:** Simple, no content features needed. **Disadvantages:** Cold-start problem, scalability issues with large datasets.
